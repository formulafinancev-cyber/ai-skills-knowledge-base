# TEMPLATE CLIENT BRIEF

## Purpose
This file defines the standard structure for a client brief inside the Formula Finance project.

Its purpose is to capture key information about a client’s situation in a way that supports:
- proposals;
- CRM work;
- analysis;
- reporting design;
- KPI definition.

## Main Principle
A client brief should answer, in a structured way:
- who the client is;
- what their business looks like;
- what problems they have;
- what they want;
- what constraints exist.

It should be short enough to read and rich enough to be useful.

## Standard Client Brief Structure

### 1. Basic Information
- Company name  
- Business type / sector  
- Size (e.g., small / medium / larger; or approximate scale)  
- Location (if relevant)  

### 2. Business Overview
A short description of what the business does in practical terms.

Focus on:
- how it operates;
- main revenue drivers;
- basic structure of operations.

### 3. Current Reporting and Visibility Situation
Describe how the client currently sees their business in terms of numbers and reports.

Questions to cover:
- What reports do they currently have (if any)?
- How regularly do they get them?
- Who prepares them?
- How confident are they in these reports?

### 4. Problems and Pains
List the main problems the client experiences.

Examples:
- lack of clear financial visibility;
- inconsistent or late reports;
- unclear KPIs;
- too many spreadsheets and no structure;
- difficulty understanding what is really happening in the business.

This section should be specific and concrete where possible.

### 5. Goals and Expectations
Describe what the client wants to achieve.

Examples:
- clearer management reporting;
- better financial understanding;
- structured dashboards;
- planning and plan-vs-fact visibility;
- a regular management analytics process.

This section should reflect how the client sees “success”.

### 6. Data and Systems Situation
Outline what data and systems the client currently has.

Examples:
- accounting systems;
- operational systems;
- exports or spreadsheets;
- known data issues.

The goal is to understand how realistic implementation will be.

### 7. Decision-Maker and Stakeholders
Describe who is involved in the decision and who will use the results.

Examples:
- owner / CEO;
- finance lead;
- operations lead;
- other key managers.

This helps align reporting and KPIs to real users.

### 8. Constraints and Risks
List known constraints.

Examples:
- limited time or attention from key people;
- unstable data sources;
- low internal support resources;
- urgency or deadlines;
- budget sensitivity.

This helps make proposals and plans realistic.

### 9. Possible Service Fit
Briefly outline which Formula Finance service directions appear relevant.

Examples:
- management reporting setup;
- KPI logic definition;
- dashboards and views;
- planning / plan-vs-fact support;
- analytical interpretation support.

This connects the brief to the service map.

### 10. Notes for Proposal / Next Steps
Add any notes that may be useful for:

- preparing a proposal;
- planning a diagnostic phase;
- structuring the first reporting steps;
- deciding on the sequence of work.

This section should keep the focus on what to do with the information.

## Quality Rules
A client brief should:
- be clear and structured;
- avoid long unbroken text blocks;
- stay focused on what matters for reporting, analytics, and decision support;
- avoid unnecessary technical or legal detail unless directly relevant.

## Role Use
This template is useful for:
- Colleague (helping fill or draft a brief);
- CRM Agent (turning conversations into structured records);
- Proposal Agent (building proposals from a brief);
- Analyst and Metrics / KPI Agent (understanding context);
- Coordinator (planning next steps).

## Final Principle
A client brief should act as a compact but powerful summary of the client’s situation, pains, goals, and constraints, directly usable for proposals and analytical work.
