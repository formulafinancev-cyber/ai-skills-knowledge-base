# TEMPLATE REPORT

## Purpose
This file defines a standard structure for an analytical or management report inside the Formula Finance project.

Its purpose is to give a reusable pattern for reports that connect numbers, KPIs, and interpretation in a clear way.

## Main Principle
A report should not just list numbers.

A good report should help a reader understand:
- what is happening;
- what is important;
- what has changed;
- what might require attention;
- what could be done next.

## Standard Report Structure

### 1. Title and Period
- Report title  
- Reporting period (e.g., month, quarter, custom range)  
- Date of preparation  

### 2. Context
Short context for the report.

Examples:
- regular management report;
- special focus report (e.g., performance, cash focus, etc.);
- follow-up to a previous period.

### 3. Key Highlights (Executive Summary)
A compact summary of the most important points.

Focus on:
- 3–7 key facts or findings;
- what changed versus the previous period;
- any critical signals.

This section should be readable quickly.

### 4. KPI Overview
Structured section with the main KPIs for the period.

Elements:
- list or table of key KPIs;
- short interpretations in plain language;
- indication of direction vs previous period (e.g., improved / worsened / stable).

The exact KPIs should align with the KPI Dictionary and Reporting Structure.

### 5. Financial View (If Applicable)
A section that focuses on financial metrics.

Examples:
- revenue-related views;
- cost-related views;
- margin-related views;
- plan vs fact if available.

The level of detail can vary depending on the report purpose.

### 6. Operational / Performance View (If Applicable)
A section that focuses on operational aspects.

Examples:
- relevant operational KPIs;
- volumes, throughput, or other key activity indicators;
- efficiency or performance metrics.

The goal is to connect operations to outcomes.

### 7. Trends and Changes
A section that highlights notable trends.

Examples:
- movements in key indicators;
- emerging patterns;
- deviations from expectations.

This section should highlight “what is moving, and how”.

### 8. Interpretation and Insights
A narrative section that explains:

- what the numbers might mean;
- what is likely driving them;
- which effects are probably structural vs temporary;
- what deserves closer attention.

This is where analysis is turned into plain-language understanding.

### 9. Risks and Points of Attention
A section that explicitly lists:

- potential risks;
- areas of concern;
- early warning signs;
- places where more information is needed.

This helps prevent reports from becoming purely positive or purely neutral.

### 10. Opportunities and Positive Signals
A section that points out:

- positive developments;
- opportunities visible in the data;
- areas where performance is promising;
- ideas for building on what works.

This balances risk-focused thinking with opportunity-focused thinking.

### 11. Recommended Next Steps
Concrete suggestions or options for what the business could do next.

Examples:
- areas to investigate further;
- focus topics for management discussion;
- structural changes to reporting;
- follow-up analyses.

The section should not become a long theoretical essay.  
It should offer practical, readable suggestions.

## Tone and Style
Reports should be:
- clear;
- structured;
- calm;
- professional;
- practical.

They should avoid:
- dramatic language;
- hype;
- overly technical explanation with no business meaning.

## Role Use
This template is useful for:
- Analyst;
- Metrics / KPI Agent;
- Colleague (for structured summaries based on reports);
- Coordinator (for planning follow-up work);
- Proposal Agent (for explaining report content in offers).

## Final Principle
A Formula Finance report should help a reader quickly orient in what is happening and where attention is needed, in a structured, clear, and calm way.
